Unit-1

Scientific Investigation and Ethics

Assignment Formative: Unit-1 Initial Post

Top-down logic, often known as deductive reasoning, starts with a basic premise and applies it to particular situations (Copi, Cohen, & McMahon, 2016). It entails beginning with accepted facts, presumptions, or hypotheses and drawing logical, sound inferences from them. The syllogism that states that all men are mortal (premise), Socrates is a man (premise), and as a result, Socrates is mortal (conclusion) is a famous example.
According to Copi et al. (2016), inductive reasoning, often known as bottom-up logic, draws general principles from particular observations or cases. In this method, more generalisations and hypotheses are developed from more precise observations. For instance, one can draw the conclusion that the sun will always rise in the morning after witnessing that it does so every morning.
